## 1. Executive Verdict

**Aurelia presents as a premium clinic and converts like a commodity one.** The brand surface (photography, space, tone) signals high-trust aesthetic care; the conversion surface (proof, practitioner visibility, pricing, booking) withholds the exact things a cautious, high-value patient needs to say yes. Demand is arriving and leaking before booking.

**Seven blunt findings:**
1. The homepage leads with superlatives ("premier," "luxury") and withholds proof — strong language, thin evidence. **The proof gap is the conversion gap.**
2. The practitioner is nearly invisible on the pages patients land on. For needle-based care, "who is treating me" is the first trust question, and the site does not answer it early.
3. No before/after proof is visible on service pages. **[PLACEHOLDER — confirm consent status of any real assets before use.]**
4. Pricing is gated behind "book a consultation." For a discretionary, anxious purchase, this is friction, not exclusivity.
5. Local trust signals (review count, recency, response) are not surfaced on-page; the business likely under-shows reputation it may already have. **[PLACEHOLDER — pull real GBP signals.]**
6. Service pages read as a generic menu identical to nearby competitors; positioning does not differentiate.
7. The booking CTA sits far from any trust-building element, so the patient is asked to commit at the moment of least confidence.

**What's working:** the brand *aesthetic* is credible and premium; the offer surface is clear about *what* is sold; the owner's underlying credentials are a genuine asset **[PLACEHOLDER — verify from public professional profile].**

**What's weak:** proof visibility, practitioner presence, price transparency, and on-page trust near the CTA.

**Biggest revenue/trust leak:** the **proof leak** — premium claims with no visible before/after or practitioner evidence. This is where interested patients quietly bounce to a competitor who *showed the work*.

**The one thing to fix first:** put consented before/after proof and the practitioner's face + credentials on the homepage and service pages this week. Highest impact, lowest effort, requires no new ad spend.

## 4. Founder / Operator Read

*Framed as public professional positioning signals — how the operator's credibility is (and isn't) reflected in public content. Not personal profiling.*

- **Credibility signals present:** owner-operator with clinical background **[PLACEHOLDER — confirm from public professional profile]**. This is the strongest under-used asset in the business.

- **Missing authority signals on-site:** the practitioner's name, face, credentials, and point of view are absent from the high-traffic pages. The site describes *services* when patients are choosing *a person*.
- **How the background should be reflected:** lead with the practitioner on the homepage; a short, specific bio (training, philosophy on natural results, years in practice) near the primary CTA; a founder note or short video.
- **What should not be overclaimed:** avoid superlatives ("best," "premier," "top") and any outcome guarantee. Let specific, verifiable credentials carry the authority instead of adjectives.
